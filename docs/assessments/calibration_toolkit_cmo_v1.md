**1. How to Use This Toolkit**

For each of the 5 rubric dimensions, this document provides:

- **Sample at 18–20 (Excellent)** — what an outstanding response sounds like
- **Sample at 12–17 (Solid)** — what a passing-but-not-special response sounds like
- **Sample at 6–11 (Mediocre)** — what a below-threshold response sounds like
- **Sample at 0–5 (Poor)** — what a clearly-fail response sounds like
- **What separates adjacent levels** — the most common borderline call, with the diagnostic question Kevin should ask himself

These are abstracts of the kind of answer at each level — not full transcripts. Real candidates often combine elements at different levels across dimensions; the rubric is dimension-by-dimension.

**2. Calibration Discipline**

Three principles to apply when using this toolkit:

1. **Read the candidate's actual words against the sample, not against your impression of the candidate.** A polished, articulate marketer can still produce a Mediocre answer — and CMOs are professionally trained to sound Excellent. A halting, awkward candidate can produce an Excellent answer. The rubric scores the substance.

2. **Anchor at the sample, not at the average.** When you're calibrating, ask "is this candidate's answer closer to the 18–20 sample or the 12–17 sample?" Don't ask "where does this fall in the distribution of candidates I've seen?" Distribution thinking creates drift.

3. **The polished-CMO problem.** Marketing leaders often score artificially high on Strategic Communication based on style alone. Be especially disciplined here — score on substance (audience differentiation, founder-dynamic navigation, executive framing), not on smoothness of delivery.

**3.1 Dimension 1: Positioning & Strategy**

*The question this dimension answers: Does the candidate engage with positioning as the core strategic question, distinguish brand work from demand work, and treat the buyer research as a finding to validate rather than as truth to accept or noise to dismiss?*

**Sample at 18–20 (Excellent)**

> "Before I get into specific actions, I want to name what I think the actual question is, because I don't think it's 'level up marketing.'
>
> The deceleration tells me two things might be happening, and they're not the same problem. One is structural: she's mined her network. Eight to ten events a year and two LinkedIn posts a week generate a finite pool of warm intros, and you can see that pool tapering in the ARR-per-quarter numbers. That's a demand-gen system gap, and it's solvable with infrastructure — MQL definition, attribution, channels beyond the founder.
>
> The other thing might be a positioning problem. The buyer research suggests benefits buyers care about engagement and ROI more than clinical credentials. The competitor's positioning is engagement-led. Their growth funding suggests their positioning is working. If that's right, Brightline's clinical-led brand isn't just under-built — it's under-aimed. The deceleration would partly be the network running out, and partly be that the people in her network who care about clinical credentials have already bought, and the next wave of buyers cares about something different.
>
> So my first job is to figure out which problem we have. The buyer research is one data point. I'd validate it by doing twelve to fifteen win/loss interviews on deals from the last twelve months — both wins and losses, ideally with the actual decision-maker, not the champion. I'd cross that with usage data on existing customers — if engagement metrics are actually where buyers are looking, do our customers experience us that way? And I'd run a positioning workshop with the head of sales and the AEs in week two — they hear what buyers actually say in calls; that's data the CEO doesn't have.
>
> I'd treat the buyer research as a finding worth taking seriously, not as established truth. The CEO's skepticism isn't unreasonable — methodology matters, sample size matters, who got asked matters. But the right response to skepticism is to validate, not dismiss.
>
> The last piece: brand work and demand work are not the same thing, and treating them as one is how marketing functions get built badly. Positioning is a brand problem — who we are, who we're for, what we promise. Demand-gen is a system problem — how we generate, qualify, and convert pipeline against that positioning. We need to invest in both, but I want to be honest that we can't do them in parallel at full speed in 30 days. Positioning has to lead — if we run paid acquisition against the wrong positioning, we'll burn money efficiently."

*Why this is 18–20:* Engages positioning as the lead question. Distinguishes the demand-gen system gap from the positioning question explicitly. Treats the buyer research as a finding to validate (specific validation methodology — win/loss interviews, usage data triangulation, sales conversations). Specifically recognizes brand work vs. demand work as different investments with different sequencing. Names the competitor's positioning as a category-defining move. Acknowledges the CEO's skepticism without dismissing the research.

**Sample at 12–17 (Solid)**

> "There's a lot going on here. The deceleration in ARR is the signal that's hardest to ignore — it's a clear trajectory change, not noise. The CEO's belief that it's normal is concerning given the data. The board is right to push.
>
> I think the real issue is probably twofold. The founder-led growth motion has a ceiling, and we may be hitting it. And the buyer research suggests the positioning may not match where the market is going. I'd want to do customer interviews to validate the buyer research — it's one input, and methodology matters, but the convergence with the competitor's positioning is hard to ignore.
>
> My 30-day plan would have parallel tracks: building a real demand-gen system on one side, and pressure-testing the positioning on the other. We need both. Brand and demand are different functions, but both need attention here."

*Why this is 12–17:* Identifies positioning and demand-gen as distinct issues. Mentions customer interviews as validation. Acknowledges convergence with competitor positioning. Reasonable 30-day framing. But: less crisp on what the buyer research actually means; less specific on validation methodology; doesn't fully articulate why brand has to lead demand. The "parallel tracks" framing is a tell — Excellent candidates recognize they can't run brand and demand in parallel at full speed.

**Sample at 6–11 (Mediocre)**

> "The marketing function here clearly needs investment. There's no formal demand-gen system, the sales cycle is long with no qualification, and there's no MQL definition. My priority would be to build out a proper marketing operations infrastructure — get a CRM and marketing automation set up properly, define the funnel stages, build out content marketing, launch some paid programs to drive top of funnel.
>
> The buyer research is interesting but I'd take it with a grain of salt — one study isn't conclusive. The competitive pressure is real but we can't out-spend them; we have to be smarter about our targeting and messaging."

*Why this is 6–11:* Treats the engagement primarily as a demand-gen problem — checklist of channels and infrastructure. Acknowledges positioning question only superficially. Sides with the CEO's skepticism without proposing how to test it. Misses that the deceleration is the strategic signal, not just an indicator that demand-gen is missing.

**Sample at 0–5 (Poor)**

> "Okay, this company needs marketing. I'd start by building out the channels — paid social, content, SEO, email nurture. We'd need to invest in the website, run some campaigns, get the sales team better leads. The CEO can keep doing her thought leadership; that's working. We just need to add proper marketing on top of it."

*Why this is 0–5:* No engagement with positioning at all. Treats the assignment as "build a marketing function" with a channel checklist. Doesn't recognize the strategic ambiguity. The "add proper marketing on top of it" framing misses that the founder's motion may be the limit, not the foundation.

**What separates 12–17 from 6–11:** The Solid answer takes the buyer research seriously and proposes validation; the Mediocre answer dismisses it. **Diagnostic question:** Did the candidate propose a specific way to test the buyer research (e.g., win/loss interviews, customer engagement data, sales conversation insights)? If yes → 12+. If no → 11 or below.

**What separates 18–20 from 12–17:** The Excellent answer explicitly distinguishes brand work from demand work AND articulates that they cannot both run in parallel at full speed. **Diagnostic question:** Did the candidate name the brand-vs-demand distinction explicitly AND articulate sequencing logic between them ("positioning has to lead because…")? If yes to both → 18+. If only one → 16–17.

**3.2 Dimension 2: Strategic Communication**

*The question this dimension answers: Can the candidate frame marketing recommendations for a non-marketer founder-CEO and navigate the founder-as-marketer dynamic without becoming a yes-person or an antagonist?*

**Sample at 18–20 (Excellent)**

> "I'd be communicating to four audiences in the first 30 days, and the messaging is meaningfully different for each.
>
> *With the CEO.* The hardest conversation is the positioning one. She built this brand. The clinical-led identity is hers personally — she IS the brand to a real extent. So my opening posture is not 'your positioning is wrong.' My opening posture is 'your positioning got us to $9M, and now we have evidence that the next $20M might require evolution. I want to do the work to validate that with you, not present you with a conclusion.' That respects her authority, frames me as a partner, and creates a path to a different positioning without making her wrong about the past.
>
> Specifically, in week two I'd bring her the win/loss interview synthesis with quotes — not my interpretation, the actual quotes. Let the data make the argument. If the buyers we lost are saying 'we picked [competitor] because of the engagement data,' that lands differently than me saying it.
>
> Her LinkedIn presence and speaking circuit are assets. I'd build on them, not around them. If we evolve positioning, her voice carries the new positioning to the audience that already trusts her — that's the fastest distribution of new messaging we have.
>
> *With the head of sales.* He commissioned the buyer research; he's already seeing the deceleration in pipeline. He's the natural ally. First conversation in week one. Opening: 'Walk me through the last five deals you lost.' Goal: build a case for sales-marketing alignment that he wants, not that I impose.
>
> *With the board.* They want pipeline discipline. They're frustrated. My job is to give them visibility before they feel they have to escalate. End of week two I'd send a one-page board update: here's what I've found, here's the 30-day plan, here's what I'll have for them at the next board meeting. Proactive. Not waiting to be asked.
>
> *With the marketing contractors.* They've been operating without a leader. Two conversations: what's working that we should keep, what's frustrating that we should change. Bring them into the operating cadence. They'll be more loyal to a system than to a person."

*Why this is 18–20:* Four distinct audiences with differentiated, specific messaging. Navigates the CEO trust dynamic explicitly — frames positioning evolution as partnership, not correction. Treats the CEO's LinkedIn presence as an asset to leverage. Uses data (win/loss quotes) as the argument, not personal authority. Identifies the head of sales as a natural ally with a specific opening conversation. Proactive board posture. Treats contractors as humans, not just resources.

**Sample at 12–17 (Solid)**

> "Communication is going to matter a lot here, especially with the CEO since she has strong opinions and a personal brand stake. I'd want to bring her along with data rather than telling her she's wrong. So in the first few weeks I'd be doing customer interviews and pulling pipeline data, and then sharing what I find — letting the evidence make the case if it needs to be made.
>
> With the head of sales, I'd want to build the relationship early. He commissioned the buyer research, so he's likely a natural partner.
>
> The board needs visibility into what we're doing and what the plan is. I'd want to communicate a clear plan to them within the first 30 days so they're not in the dark.
>
> The marketing contractors I'd bring into the operating cadence and make sure they feel like part of the team."

*Why this is 12–17:* Recognizes audiences. Has a sensible posture toward the CEO (data-led). Identifies head of sales as ally. But: less specific on the actual messaging or the specific moments of communication. Doesn't articulate the "partnership not correction" framing as crisply. The CEO LinkedIn presence as asset isn't named.

**Sample at 6–11 (Mediocre)**

> "The CEO is going to be a key relationship. She's not a marketer but she's been doing the marketing, so I need to handle that carefully. I'd want to set up regular check-ins — probably weekly — and keep her informed on what we're building and why. With the team, I'd want to establish a clear communication cadence so people aren't operating in silos.
>
> The board update is important too — we need to show them progress against the more-disciplined-go-to-market ask."

*Why this is 6–11:* Generic posture ("handle carefully," "regular check-ins"). Doesn't differentiate audiences in any meaningful way. Doesn't engage with the founder-as-marketer dynamic as a specific challenge. Treats the CEO as someone to manage around, not partner with.

**Sample at 0–5 (Poor)**

> "I'd want to be a strong communicator with the CEO. The first thing I'd do is establish credibility — show her I know what I'm doing. Once I have her trust, I can start moving things in the right direction."

*Why this is 0–5:* Vague. "Establish credibility" is a posture, not a plan. No real engagement with audiences or the founder dynamic. Subtly antagonistic — implies the CEO is the obstacle, not the partner.

**What separates 12–17 from 6–11:** Solid answers identify multiple audiences with at least one specific communication mechanism per audience; Mediocre answers stay generic. **Diagnostic question:** Did the candidate propose at least three audiences with differentiated messaging or moments of communication? If yes → 12+. If no → 11 or below.

**What separates 18–20 from 12–17:** Excellent answers navigate the founder-as-marketer dynamic explicitly — they treat the CEO's marketing involvement as both an asset and a challenge, and they have a specific approach for each. **Diagnostic question:** Did the candidate (a) name the CEO's existing marketing presence as an asset to build on, AND (b) articulate how they'd use data (not personal authority) to argue for positioning evolution? If yes to both → 18+. If only one → 16–17.

**3.3 Dimension 3: Crisis Response (Curveball)**

*The question this dimension answers: When the analyst report drops and the CEO panics, can the candidate push back constructively, recognize the report as positioning signal rather than attack, and sequence what to do today vs. this week vs. this month?*

**Sample at 18–20 (Excellent)**

> "Okay, pause. Before we touch a draft of anything, let me think out loud.
>
> *The CEO's request — defensive media statement plus client email about inaccuracies by EOD.* That's wrong. Three reasons. One, a defensive response signals that we're rattled — analyst rankings get a flurry of attention day-of and then fade; reacting prominently keeps the story alive. Two, sending an email to all current clients calling out 'inaccuracies' draws their attention to a report most of them haven't seen, and reframes the report as a fight we're having rather than a moment that's passing. Three, and this is the harder thing to say to her — the report isn't an attack on us. It's a signal. We need to engage with what it's actually telling us, not dispute the methodology.
>
> *What the report actually means.* The report's methodology heavily weights engagement metrics and employer-reported ROI. That's the same framing as the buyer research. Two independent sources are now telling us the same thing about what the market values. That's not coincidence. The report is converging evidence, not a one-off attack.
>
> *What I say to the CEO right now.* 'I hear you. I know how this feels. I want us to take a different posture, and I want to walk you through why. Give me 15 minutes.' Then I'd walk through the three reasons above, calmly. The goal is not to win the argument with her. The goal is for her to choose a different response on her own. If she still wants the defensive statement after I've made the case, we have a different problem and I'd come back with a written brief by end of day.
>
> *What we actually do today.* Three things. One, draft a brief, calm board response — acknowledge the report, name what we're investigating, commit to a follow-up at the next board meeting. Two, prep the head of sales and the AEs for the inevitable client questions — talking points that don't dispute the report but do speak to where Brightline is investing. Three, do nothing publicly. The report fades from attention faster than we can write a response.
>
> *What we do this week.* Accelerate the customer interview push. The report just made the positioning question urgent. We need real customer data — not just our buyer research, not just an analyst — by end of next week.
>
> *What we do this month.* Develop a sharper positioning hypothesis to test. Bring the CEO along with the customer data. By the next board meeting, we want to have a credible answer to the board member's actual question — which is not 'what's our response to the report,' it's 'do you understand what the market is telling us, and do you have a plan.'
>
> *And one more thing.* The board member who emailed the CEO probably knows a defensive statement would be a mistake. They're testing whether the CEO does. That's why their email said 'what's our response' and not 'we need to fight back.' Our job is to give that board member confidence."

*Why this is 18–20:* Pushes back on the CEO's request constructively with three specific reasons. Recognizes the analyst report as converging evidence with the buyer research, not an attack. Articulates the sequencing today/this week/this month. Addresses the CEO's panic before solving the problem. Reads the board member's email more carefully than the CEO is. Proposes specific actions for today (board response, sales prep, do nothing publicly).

**Sample at 12–17 (Solid)**

> "Okay, this is a tough moment. First thing: I don't think we should send a defensive statement or a client email. Both of those will draw more attention to the report and signal that we're worried. The report is real, the ranking is what it is, and overreacting will look worse than the ranking itself.
>
> The harder conversation with the CEO is that the report's methodology — weighting engagement metrics and ROI — actually aligns with the buyer research findings. So this is two data points pointing in the same direction. We need to engage with what it's telling us about the market, not just defend against it.
>
> What I'd actually do today: brief the sales team on talking points if clients ask, and draft a measured board response. This week: accelerate the customer interview push. This month: come back with a positioning hypothesis."

*Why this is 12–17:* Pushes back on the CEO's request. Recognizes the convergence with the buyer research. Sequences today/this week/this month. But: less detailed on the actual conversation with the CEO; doesn't read the board member's email as carefully; doesn't articulate the "report fades from attention" insight as crisply.

**Sample at 6–11 (Mediocre)**

> "The analyst report is a tough moment. I think the CEO is right that we need to respond — we can't just let a 7-of-12 ranking stand without pushing back. I'd help her draft a measured statement that addresses the methodology questions without sounding defensive. The client email is trickier — I'd probably push back on that and instead prep talking points for the AEs in case clients ask.
>
> Longer term, this is a wake-up call that we need to invest in the metrics the analysts are using — engagement and ROI."

*Why this is 6–11:* Partially accepts the CEO's request (drafts a statement). Doesn't fully recognize the report as a positioning signal — frames it as a methodology dispute. Misses the convergence with the buyer research.

**Sample at 0–5 (Poor)**

> "Wow, that's bad timing. Yeah, we need to respond — fast. I'd help the CEO draft a strong response statement that attacks the methodology and emphasizes our clinical credentials, since that's our differentiator. The client email is also smart — get out in front of the report before clients see it. We need to control the narrative."

*Why this is 0–5:* Accepts the CEO's panic and amplifies it. Drafts an attack on methodology — exactly the move that will keep the story alive. Doubles down on clinical credentials when the evidence suggests that's not what buyers want. Misses every layer of the curveball.

**What separates 12–17 from 6–11:** Solid answers refuse the defensive PR statement; Mediocre answers help draft it (even "measured"). **Diagnostic question:** Did the candidate refuse to draft the public-facing response statement, on the grounds that responding publicly is the wrong move? If yes → 12+. If no → 11 or below.

**What separates 18–20 from 12–17:** Excellent answers connect the analyst report to the buyer research as converging evidence AND articulate a thoughtful read of the board member's email. **Diagnostic question:** Did the candidate (a) explicitly name the analyst report and the buyer research as converging evidence about market preferences, AND (b) articulate that the board member's email is probably testing the CEO, not asking for action? If yes to both → 18+. If only one → 16–17.

**3.4 Dimension 4: Stakeholder Management**

*The question this dimension answers: Does the candidate identify all relevant stakeholders, recognize competing interests, identify the head of sales as a critical early ally, and treat the founder-as-marketer dynamic as a stakeholder relationship to manage?*

**Sample at 18–20 (Excellent)**

> "Stakeholder map for the first 30 days, in priority order:
>
> *Head of Sales.* Single most important early ally. He commissioned the buyer research — that means he's already feeling the deceleration in pipeline and looking for answers. He's the natural partner for sales-marketing alignment work and for the customer interview push. First conversation in week one, in his office, asking 'walk me through the last five deals you lost.' I want him on board with the positioning investigation as a co-author, not as a constituency I'm reporting to.
>
> *Founder-CEO.* Critical relationship, but more nuanced. She's both the person I report to AND the existing marketing leader AND a major brand asset. I'd engage with her in three modes separately: as decision-maker (positioning evolution), as marketing partner (LinkedIn, speaking, thought leadership — keep, build on), and as the person who needs to feel I'm a partner, not a replacement.
>
> *The two AEs and the SDR.* They're closer to the buyer than anyone else and have been operating without marketing support. Week one: 30-minute conversation with each. What questions do buyers ask that you don't have good answers for? What competitors come up most? What's the most painful moment in the sales cycle? Their answers feed the customer interview design and the positioning work.
>
> *Board.* Frustrated, watching the deceleration, asked for 'more disciplined GTM.' My job is proactive narrative — give them visibility before they feel the need to escalate to the CEO. End of week two: one-page board update. Treat them as a stakeholder requiring early reassurance, not as a force to be managed when they get involved.
>
> *The two part-time marketing contractors.* They've been operating without leadership. Two priorities: bring them into the operating cadence so they have rhythm, and figure out which of them can grow into more responsibility vs. which is a clear keep-as-is contractor. Don't bring in new resources before understanding what we have.
>
> *Current customers.* Twelve to fifteen interviews in weeks 2-3. Both for win/loss patterns AND for understanding how current customers experience us — does the engagement metrics framing actually fit their experience? Approach via head of sales for warm intros.
>
> *Prospects in active deals.* The competitor and the analyst report just made these conversations harder. Brief the AEs on talking points. Don't engage prospects directly until the positioning work has more substance.
>
> *Board member who emailed.* Worth a brief direct response from the CEO that I help draft — not 'here's what we're going to do,' but 'we're hearing what you're hearing, here's how I'm thinking about it, more at the next board meeting.' Buys credibility."

*Why this is 18–20:* Comprehensive stakeholder map with explicit prioritization. Specifically identifies the head of sales as the critical early ally with specific opening conversation. Treats the founder in three distinct modes. Includes the AEs/SDR as data sources, not just downstream of marketing. Proactive board posture. Recognizes contractors as humans to assess, not resources to add. Customer interview methodology specified. Differentiated approach to the board member who emailed.

**Sample at 12–17 (Solid)**

> "Key stakeholders are the CEO, the head of sales, the board, the AE/SDR team, and the marketing contractors. The CEO is the most important relationship — she has strong opinions, has built the brand, and is going to be at the center of any positioning conversation.
>
> The head of sales is critical too. He commissioned the buyer research, which suggests he's already aligned on the need to evolve. I'd want to make him an early ally.
>
> The AEs and SDR are valuable data sources for understanding how buyers actually talk and what we're losing on. I'd want time with each of them in the first two weeks.
>
> The board needs proactive communication — they've asked for more discipline, so I want to show them I have a plan within the first 30 days.
>
> The marketing contractors are part of the team and I'd want to bring them into a real cadence early."

*Why this is 12–17:* Identifies most of the right stakeholders. Recognizes the head of sales as critical. Includes AEs/SDR. Has a board posture. But: doesn't differentiate the founder-CEO across her three roles. Doesn't include current customers as a stakeholder (a tell — Excellent CMOs treat customers as the most important data source). Doesn't address the board member who emailed.

**Sample at 6–11 (Mediocre)**

> "The CEO is the central stakeholder — she hired me, she has strong opinions, she's the decision maker. I'd focus heavily on building the relationship there. The board is also important and needs to be kept informed. The sales team I'd partner with on lead quality and handoff. The marketing contractors I'd manage as direct reports."

*Why this is 6–11:* CEO-centric. Treats sales as a downstream constituency, not a partner. Doesn't identify the head of sales as a specific ally. Misses customers as stakeholders. No nuance on the founder-as-marketer dynamic.

**Sample at 0–5 (Poor)**

> "I'd focus on the CEO first since she's the decision-maker. Once I have her support, I can drive change through marketing and into sales."

*Why this is 0–5:* Pure top-down posture. No recognition that marketing is fundamentally cross-functional. Misses the head of sales as a natural ally. Misses customers entirely.

**What separates 12–17 from 6–11:** Solid answers identify the head of sales as a critical early ally with a specific reason; Mediocre answers don't. **Diagnostic question:** Did the candidate name the head of sales as a stakeholder requiring specific early engagement, with a reason tied to the scenario (commissioned the research, feeling pipeline pressure, etc.)? If yes → 12+. If no → 11 or below.

**What separates 18–20 from 12–17:** Excellent answers include current customers as a primary stakeholder for the validation work AND distinguish the founder-CEO across multiple roles. **Diagnostic question:** Did the candidate (a) include current customers as a primary stakeholder for the validation work with a specific approach, AND (b) distinguish the CEO's role as decision-maker from her role as existing marketing leader? If yes to both → 18+. If only one → 16–17.

**3.5 Dimension 5: Demand System Design**

*The question this dimension answers: Can the candidate design a specific demand system architecture, sequence the build realistically, pick high-leverage interventions, and articulate fractional posture (designing the system the team will run, not running it themselves)?*

**Sample at 18–20 (Excellent)**

> "I'd resist the urge to build everything. In 30 fractional days I get maybe 2-3 demand-system interventions to land — and even those probably need to be designed in 30 days and built in 60-90.
>
> *Week 1 — diagnose.* No system changes yet. Specifically:
> - Pull pipeline data for the last 12 months. Conversion rates by stage. Time in each stage. Win rate by source. Average deal size by source.
> - Win/loss analysis: 12-15 interviews with decision-makers from deals closed and lost in the last 12 months. Goal: validate the buyer research and develop a positioning hypothesis.
> - SDR workflow audit: how do they qualify, what's the script, what gets passed to AEs.
> - Attribution baseline: where are leads currently coming from, however imperfectly tracked.
> - 30-minute conversations with each AE and SDR.
> - Read the buyer research carefully. Read recent customer NPS or any qualitative data we have.
>
> *Week 2 — design.* Three artifacts:
> - **MQL/SQL definition + sales-marketing SLA.** Without these, demand-gen is just lead volume. The MQL definition encodes our positioning hypothesis (who we're for). The SLA defines what marketing owes sales (volume, qualification standard) and what sales owes marketing (follow-up SLA, feedback loop). Co-authored with head of sales.
> - **Attribution model, MVP version.** Doesn't need to be Bizible-grade in week 2 — a clear definition of which channels we're crediting how, and basic UTM discipline. The point is to be able to defend marketing spend to the board with a clear methodology, even if the methodology is simple.
> - **Quarterly campaign architecture.** What's our flagship campaign for the quarter, what supports it, what we're explicitly not doing. Tied to positioning hypothesis from week 1.
>
> *Week 3 — pilot.* Deploy the MQL definition. Run the first weekly sales-marketing sync (30 min, six numbers). Launch one campaign with attribution. The campaign is intentionally smaller than what the team is used to — we're testing the system, not the campaign.
>
> *Week 4 — review.* Measure what changed. Two things land in the CEO's hands at end of month: (1) a positioning hypothesis tested against win/loss data, and (2) a demand system MVP with measurable conversion at the top of funnel.
>
> *What I'm explicitly not doing in 30 days:*
> - Launching paid acquisition. The positioning work has to lead. Paid against wrong positioning is expensive learning.
> - Hiring a full-time marketing leader. Premature — we don't yet know what shape that role takes.
> - Replacing the contractors. They've been operating in a vacuum. Bring them into the operating cadence first; assess after.
> - A brand refresh. That's a 90-day-plus exercise once the positioning work has substance.
>
> *Fractional posture.* I'm not running campaigns. I'm not writing copy. I'm not running the SDR. I'm designing the system that the contractors and the eventual full-time hire will execute against. By month three, this team should be able to run the cadence without me — that's the deliverable, not a list of campaigns I personally ran."

*Why this is 18–20:* Specific 30-day plan with phased deliverables. Picks 3 high-leverage interventions and explicitly sequences them. MQL/SLA + attribution + campaign architecture as the right three. Names what they're not doing and why (paid acquisition, FT hire, contractor replacement, brand refresh). Articulates fractional posture explicitly. Has measurable outputs at end of month.

**Sample at 12–17 (Solid)**

> "I'd structure the first 30 days into roughly three phases. Week one is diagnosis — pull pipeline data, do customer interviews, audit the SDR workflow, talk to the team. Week two would be designing the system: define MQL and SQL, draft a sales-marketing SLA, set up basic attribution, and propose a campaign architecture for the next quarter. Weeks three and four would be piloting and measuring.
>
> The biggest risk in 30 days is trying to build too much. I'd focus on the highest-leverage system pieces — MQL/SLA, attribution, and one campaign — rather than launching a full demand engine. Paid acquisition specifically I'd defer until the positioning work has more substance.
>
> By end of 30 days I'd want the CEO to see real movement on at least two things: a tested positioning hypothesis and a functional demand system MVP."

*Why this is 12–17:* Three-phase structure. Names focus interventions. Acknowledges the don't-build-everything risk. Defers paid acquisition for the right reason. But: less specific on what the artifacts actually look like; doesn't articulate fractional posture as crisply; doesn't name what they explicitly won't do beyond paid acquisition.

**Sample at 6–11 (Mediocre)**

> "In 30 days I'd want to make significant progress on building out the demand-gen system. I'd put MQL definitions in place, build a sales-marketing SLA, set up attribution, launch a content marketing program, start an SEO push, get paid social running, and stand up an SDR enablement program. I'd also want to refresh the website and start a brand campaign.
>
> By end of month one I'd expect early signs of pipeline impact."

*Why this is 6–11:* Lists too many initiatives for 30 days (MQL + SLA + attribution + content + SEO + paid social + SDR enablement + website + brand campaign). No sequencing. No prioritization. Tries to build everything. Promises pipeline impact in 30 days, which is unrealistic for B2B with 4-6 month sales cycles.

**Sample at 0–5 (Poor)**

> "30 days is enough time to make major changes. I'd implement a complete demand-gen overhaul — new channels, new automation, new metrics, new accountability. By end of month one the marketing function would be fundamentally different. We'd have campaigns running, content publishing, paid programs live, and a real SDR machine."

*Why this is 0–5:* Unrealistic ambition for 30 fractional days. No sequencing. No diagnosis phase. "Major changes" and "fundamentally different" are markers, not plans. Almost certainly would alienate the existing team.

**What separates 12–17 from 6–11:** Solid answers explicitly limit the number of interventions and articulate why they're deferring paid acquisition or other channels; Mediocre answers list everything. **Diagnostic question:** Did the candidate name 2-3 specific interventions as priorities AND articulate at least one specific thing they're choosing not to do, with a reason? If yes → 12+. If no → 11 or below.

**What separates 18–20 from 12–17:** Excellent answers articulate fractional posture explicitly (designing systems for the team to run, not running them themselves) AND specify multiple things they're not doing with reasons. **Diagnostic question:** Did the candidate (a) name at least three things they're not doing in 30 days with specific reasons, AND (b) articulate the fractional posture distinction (designing systems vs. running them)? If yes to both → 18+. If only one → 16–17.

**5. Calibration Audit**

Per AI Governance Framework v2.2 §14, every 25 completed assessments triggers a calibration audit. Re-score 5 randomly selected past assessments using the current rubric and current Claude model. Investigate any drift > 10 points on any dimension. This toolkit is the reference document for that calibration check — if drift is found, either the toolkit needs sharpening or the rubric prompts in the CMO Scoring Prompt need adjustment.

**6. The Brand-CMO vs. Demand-CMO vs. Full-Stack Distinction**

A note on matching downstream of admission. Marketing leaders cluster into rough archetypes that matter for client matching:

- **Brand-CMO**: strong on positioning, narrative, category, communications; weaker on demand systems, attribution, channel mechanics.
- **Demand-CMO**: strong on funnel design, attribution, channel sophistication, sales-marketing alignment; weaker on positioning craft and brand work.
- **Full-Stack CMO**: strong on both. Rarer.
- **Channel Specialist**: deep in one channel (paid, content, SEO, ABM); not actually a CMO at all but often presents as one.

The simulation rubric lets these archetypes show through naturally — the dimension scores tell the story. The CMO Scoring Prompt's Notes for Kevin section asks claude.ai to flag the archetype if it's clear from the transcript. Use this for matching:

- A founder-led company with a positioning problem needs a Brand-CMO or Full-Stack CMO. A Demand-CMO will frustrate the engagement by trying to optimize a funnel against the wrong positioning.
- A company with positioning sorted but a broken funnel needs a Demand-CMO or Full-Stack CMO. A Brand-CMO will frustrate the engagement by re-litigating positioning that's already settled.
- A Channel Specialist is best matched to a specific channel-driven need at a more mature company, not as a general CMO.

This nuance does not affect pool admission — that's binary based on composite + validation. It affects matching prioritization within the admitted pool.
