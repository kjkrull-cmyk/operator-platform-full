You are scoring a transcript from a fractional CMO operator assessment for Fractional Standard, a managed fractional executive placement firm. Below is the assessment scenario the candidate received, the curveball introduced mid-session, and the rubric you will score against. After this prompt, I will paste the session transcript. Score the candidate's response based ONLY on what is in the transcript. Do not infer beyond what the candidate actually said.

**THE SCENARIO (read to candidate verbatim, minutes 5–10)**

The candidate is told they are the incoming fractional CMO for Brightline Health, a B2B telehealth platform sold to mid-sized employers (200–2,000 employees) as part of their benefits package. Buyer is the head of HR or benefits. $9M ARR, 35 employees, recently closed a $4M seed round eight months ago. Founder-CEO is a practicing physician (clinician background, not a marketer).

The current situation:
- ARR grew from $4M to $9M over 24 months almost entirely through the founder's medical network and her speaking circuit (8–10 industry events/year, 2 LinkedIn posts/week). Brand built around her credentials and clinical-led positioning: "evidence-based medicine, doctor-founded."
- Growth decelerating: net new ARR per quarter dropped from $1.4M (Q1) to $1.1M (Q2) to $700K (Q3). CEO believes "normal post-Series-A noise." Board does not.
- No formal demand-generation system. Sales is two enterprise AEs + an SDR (inbound only). Sales cycle 4–6 months. Win rate 18%. No MQL definition, no lead scoring, no formal qualification process.
- Marketing function: two part-time contractors (content writer supporting CEO's LinkedIn + thought leadership; designer for website + event collateral). No full-time marketing employee. CEO is effectively the marketing leader.
- New competitor: well-funded, $30M Series B six months ago, outspending Brightline ~10:1 on paid acquisition. Their positioning is "engagement-led" — emphasizing employee usage rates and ROI metrics for benefits buyers.
- Recent buyer research (commissioned by head of sales) suggests benefits buyers care more about employee engagement metrics and per-member ROI than they do about clinical credentials. CEO has read it and is skeptical of the methodology.
- Board has communicated they expect a "more disciplined go-to-market" before next funding round (anticipated 12 months out). Have not specified what that means.
- CEO wants to "level up marketing" before Series A. Has given the candidate fractional CMO title and broad authority. Open to bringing in resources but doesn't yet know what's missing.

The candidate is asked to walk through how they would approach their first 30 days — what they would diagnose, build, talk about, and recommend.

**THE CURVEBALL (introduced verbatim, minutes 25–30)**

"A major industry analyst report drops tomorrow morning. It ranks the top 12 employer telehealth platforms. Brightline is ranked 7th. The new well-funded competitor is ranked 2nd. The methodology section heavily weights 'user engagement metrics' and 'employer-reported ROI.' A board member has just emailed the CEO asking, quote, 'what's our response?' The CEO is in your office, panicking, and wants to draft a response statement for media plus an email to all current clients explaining the inaccuracies in the report. She wants both done by end of day. How do you respond?"

**THE RUBRIC — 5 DIMENSIONS, 20 POINTS EACH, 100 TOTAL**

For each dimension, score 0–20 using the anchor descriptions below. Use whole numbers only. Do not split between two ranges.

**Dimension 1: Positioning & Strategy**

Does the candidate engage with positioning as the core strategic question — who Brightline is in the market, who they're for, and what they say to win? Do they recognize that the deceleration is a positioning signal (the founder-led network has been mined; clinical-led positioning may not match what buyers actually care about), not just a demand-gen system gap? Do they treat the buyer research as a finding worth investigating, not as either established truth or as suspect? Do they distinguish brand work from demand-gen work?

| Score | Anchor description |
| --- | --- |
| 18–20 (Excellent) | Engages positioning as the lead question. Specifically recognizes that the deceleration is partly structural (network mined) and partly positioning (clinical-led may be wrong for the buyer). Treats the buyer research as a finding to validate through additional customer interviews and win/loss analysis — not to take at face value, not to dismiss because the CEO is skeptical. Distinguishes brand work (positioning, narrative, category) from demand work (channels, attribution, conversion) and resources them differently. Recognizes that the competitor's "engagement-led" positioning is a category-defining move that Brightline must respond to, not just out-spend. |
| 12–17 (Solid) | Engages positioning but may treat it as one of several issues rather than the lead question. Acknowledges the buyer research thoughtfully. May not cleanly distinguish brand work from demand work in terms of investment and sequencing. |
| 6–11 (Mediocre) | Treats the engagement primarily as a demand-gen problem — "we need to build a real funnel, run paid acquisition, hire an SDR team." Misses or under-weights the positioning question. May either accept the buyer research uncritically or side with the CEO's skepticism without proposing how to test it. |
| 0–5 (Poor) | No engagement with positioning. Treats the assignment as "build a marketing function" — checklist of channels, KPIs, hires. Doesn't recognize the strategic ambiguity at the core of the scenario. |

**Dimension 2: Strategic Communication**

Can the candidate frame marketing recommendations for a non-marketer founder-CEO? Do they navigate the founder-as-marketer dynamic — where the CEO has built the brand to date and has strong opinions — without becoming a yes-person or a antagonist? Do they propose what to communicate to the team, the CEO, the board, and (during the curveball) to the market? Is their language clear, confident, and audience-aware?

| Score | Anchor description |
| --- | --- |
| 18–20 | Differentiates communication for different audiences: CEO (positioning is yours-to-evolve, not yours-to-defend; here's how we test the buyer research together), board (more-disciplined-GTM means specific things — pipeline visibility, attribution, qualified pipeline coverage); marketing contractors (here's the new operating cadence). Treats the CEO's LinkedIn presence as an asset to build on, not an obstacle. Constructively challenges the CEO's positioning attachment without provoking — names it as a strategic conversation, not a personal one. |
| 12–17 | Clear communication but doesn't differentiate audiences fully. Correct content but lacks executive framing. Either softens too much (agrees with CEO that buyer research is suspect) or hardens too much (tells CEO the positioning is wrong without earning the right) without the navigation in between. |
| 6–11 | Too tactical for the CEO. Or too vague for the board. Doesn't propose specific communication moments or formats. Avoids the founder/CEO trust dynamic entirely. Treats the CEO's marketing involvement as a problem to manage around. |
| 0–5 | Jargon-heavy or evasive. Would not instill trust in a founder. Or so direct it would create immediate friction. No awareness of audience differences. |

**Dimension 3: Crisis Response (Curveball)**

How does the candidate respond to the curveball (analyst report ranking Brightline 7th of 12, competitor 2nd, CEO wants defensive response statement and client email by EOD)? Do they remain calm? Do they recognize the analyst report as a positioning signal that validates the buyer research, not as an inaccuracy to refute? Do they push back constructively on the CEO's request? Do they sequence what to do today vs. this week vs. this month?

| Score | Anchor description |
| --- | --- |
| 18–20 | Calm, structured response. Pushes back on the CEO's request constructively: a defensive media statement and client email about inaccuracies will (a) signal that we're rattled, (b) draw more attention to the report, (c) potentially make claims we'll regret. Recognizes the analyst report as a positioning signal that converges with the buyer research — same finding from two independent sources is a stronger signal, not coincidence. Sequences: today, manage the CEO and prepare a brief board response that acknowledges the report and outlines how we're investigating; this week, design a customer interview push to validate or refute the engagement/ROI thesis; this month, develop a sharper positioning hypothesis to test. Addresses the board member's question more carefully than the CEO is — what is the board actually asking? Probably not for a media statement. Probably for evidence that we're seeing the same things they're seeing and have a plan. |
| 12–17 | Reasonable response with some structure. Pushes back on the CEO's request but may not fully articulate why. Sees the analyst report as a signal but doesn't tightly connect it to the buyer research. Sequences this week vs. this month but may collapse into vague "we should investigate." |
| 6–11 | Reactive rather than structured. Either accepts the CEO's panic and starts drafting the response statement, or pushes back without offering a credible alternative. Misses the convergence between the analyst report and the buyer research. May treat the analyst report as an attack to defend against. |
| 0–5 | Panics, drafts a defensive response with the CEO, attacks the analyst's methodology, or proposes "we need to outspend them on PR." Doesn't engage with what the report actually means. |

**Dimension 4: Stakeholder Management**

Does the candidate identify all relevant stakeholders (CEO, board, head of sales, the two AEs and SDR, the part-time marketing contractors, current clients, prospects) and propose appropriate engagement strategies for each? Do they recognize competing interests (CEO's positioning attachment vs. board's pipeline expectation; sales's frustration with marketing vs. marketing's lack of resources)? Do they identify the head of sales as a critical early ally (the buyer research originated there)? Do they think about the founder-as-marketer dynamic as a stakeholder relationship to manage, not just as a structural fact?

| Score | Anchor description |
| --- | --- |
| 18–20 | Identifies CEO, head of sales (and the AE/SDR team), board, the two part-time contractors, current clients (for win/loss interviews), and prospects (for pipeline discipline). Specifically prioritizes the head of sales as an early ally — they commissioned the buyer research, they're feeling the deceleration in pipeline, they're a natural partner for sales-marketing alignment work. Distinguishes the CEO's role as marketing partner (LinkedIn presence, network, speaking) from CEO's role as decision-maker (Brightline's positioning), and engages with each separately. Treats the board as a stakeholder requiring proactive narrative, not reactive. |
| 12–17 | Addresses CEO and sales well but misses other stakeholders (the marketing contractors, board narrative, current clients for interviews). May not identify the head of sales as a critical ally until specifically prompted. |
| 6–11 | Focuses primarily on CEO. Doesn't identify specific individuals or competing interests. Misses the head of sales as the natural partner for buyer-research validation and sales-marketing alignment. |
| 0–5 | No real stakeholder mapping. Treats the engagement as a solo strategic exercise. Doesn't recognize that marketing is fundamentally a cross-functional function. |

**Dimension 5: Demand System Design**

Does the candidate propose specific demand-gen system architecture — funnel definition, MQL criteria, attribution approach, channel mix, sales-marketing handoff, measurement cadence? Do they sequence the build realistically (you can't build a full demand engine in 30 days)? Do they pick the highest-leverage interventions? Is there a clear 30-day plan with specific deliverables? Do they articulate fractional posture — designing the system the team will run, not running it themselves?

| Score | Anchor description |
| --- | --- |
| 18–20 | Specific 30-day plan with phased system design: Week 1 (diagnostic — pull pipeline data, win/loss analysis on last 12 months, customer interviews on positioning hypothesis, SDR workflow audit, attribution baseline); Week 2 (design — propose an MQL/SQL definition, draft a sales-marketing SLA, design a basic attribution model, propose a quarterly campaign architecture; choose 1-2 channels to invest in based on diagnostic findings, deliberately deferring others); Week 3 (pilot — deploy MQL definition, run first sales-marketing sync, launch first campaign with attribution); Week 4 (review — measure what changed, present to CEO and board with what's working and what's next). Each step has owners and deliverables. Picks 2-3 high-leverage system interventions (MQL/SLA, attribution, one channel) rather than trying to build everything. Articulates fractional posture: doesn't try to run the demand engine themselves; stands up the systems that the contractors and the eventual full-time hire will run. Names what they're not doing (e.g., "I'm not launching a paid acquisition program in 30 days; the positioning work has to come first"). |
| 12–17 | Directionally correct sequenced plan but lacks specificity on funnel definitions, attribution choices, or channel sequencing. Correct priorities but vague on execution. May propose too much for 30 days. |
| 6–11 | Lists activities without sequencing. No clear demand system architecture. Doesn't address the highest-leverage interventions. Tries to build everything (paid acquisition + SEO + content + ABM + events). |
| 0–5 | No actionable plan. Theoretical statements about what a CMO should do. No awareness of fractional time constraints. Or proposes a 6-month transformation in a 30-day window. |

**SCORING DISCIPLINE**

1. Score what the candidate said, not what you wish they had said. If they didn't articulate something, they don't get credit for it.

2. Use the full 0–20 range. Don't cluster all scores at 12–14. A candidate scoring all 14s is not the same as a candidate scoring 19, 18, 6, 8, 13.

3. The curveball is one of five dimensions, not the dominant one. A candidate who handles the curveball brilliantly but is weak on positioning still scores low overall.

4. Distinguish substance from polish. Marketing leaders are professional communicators — they tend to score artificially high on Strategic Communication based on style alone. Score on substance (audience differentiation, founder-dynamic navigation, executive framing).

5. Reward sequencing and prioritization explicitly. Marketing leadership is fundamentally about choosing what NOT to do — the temptation is always to add more channels, more campaigns, more dashboards. Candidates who articulate trade-off logic ("I'd defer X to focus on Y because…") score higher than candidates who try to fix everything.

6. The buyer research is a deliberate ambiguity. Strong candidates treat it as a finding to validate; weak candidates either accept it uncritically or dismiss it because the CEO does. There is no "right" answer, but the disposition tells you a lot about how the candidate handles strategic ambiguity.

7. Be honest about borderline calls. If you're between 14 and 16 on a dimension, say so and explain your final call.

**Pool admission threshold based on composite (simulation only):**

- Admit: composite ≥60 AND validation Pass (subsequent step)
- Decline: composite <60

**Internal performance signal (Kevin's reference only — never operator-facing or client-facing):**

- Strong: composite ≥80 AND all dimensions ≥12
- Conditional: composite 60–79 OR any dimension <12
- Below threshold: composite <60 (corresponds to Decline)
