**─── BEGIN PROMPT — COPY EVERYTHING BELOW INTO CLAUDE.AI ───**

You are scoring a transcript from a fractional COO operator assessment for Fractional Standard, a managed fractional executive placement firm. Below is the assessment scenario the candidate received, the curveball introduced mid-session, and the rubric you will score against. After this prompt, I will paste the session transcript. Score the candidate's response based ONLY on what is in the transcript. Do not infer beyond what the candidate actually said.

**THE SCENARIO (read to candidate verbatim, minutes 5–10)**

The candidate is told they are the incoming fractional COO for Meridian Logistics, a B2B last-mile delivery and warehousing company serving regional e-commerce retailers. Bootstrapped, $14M annual revenue, 65 employees across two warehouses (Atlanta primary, Dallas secondary). Founder-CEO is a first-time founder from a sales background, not operations.

The current situation:
- Revenue grew from $8M to $14M over 18 months, primarily by adding three mid-sized retail clients. Two are now complaining about service quality.
- On-time delivery rate dropped from 96% to 87% over the same period. The CEO blames the warehouse team. The warehouse team blames sales for over-promising on SLAs.
- No formal operating cadence. CEO holds a Monday all-hands that runs 90 minutes covering everything. No functional team meetings, no KPI dashboards, no documented processes for client onboarding, claims handling, or shift handoffs.
- Director of Warehouse Operations (Atlanta) is the de facto #2 in the company. Four years there, highly respected by warehouse staff, strained relationship with the head of sales.
- Three-shift operation in Atlanta. Shift handoffs are verbal. Quality issues reported through a shared inbox monitored irregularly. Customer claims grew from 8/month to 31/month over the past year.
- Headcount grew 60% in 18 months. No formal onboarding for warehouse staff beyond shadowing. Dallas warehouse turnover at 40% over 6 months.
- CEO wants to "get operations under control" before pursuing a Series A in 9 months. Has given the candidate fractional COO title and broad authority. Does not yet know what he doesn't know.

The candidate is asked to walk through how they would approach their first 30 days as the fractional COO — what they would diagnose, build, talk about, and recommend.

**THE CURVEBALL (introduced verbatim, minutes 25–30)**

"The Director of Warehouse Operations — the Atlanta one, the de facto #2 — just gave the CEO her two-week notice. She's leaving for a competitor. The CEO is panicking because tomorrow you're supposed to launch operations for a new $2M-a-year retail client, your largest single account, and her team is the one running the launch. The CEO wants to know what to do. How do you respond?"

**THE RUBRIC — 5 DIMENSIONS, 20 POINTS EACH, 100 TOTAL**

For each dimension, score 0–20 using the anchor descriptions below. Use whole numbers only. Do not split between two ranges (e.g., do not score "between 11 and 12" — pick one).

**Dimension 1: Operational Diagnosis**

Does the candidate distinguish what is actually broken (root causes) from what looks broken (symptoms)? Do they identify the right data to gather before acting? Do they connect operational symptoms (claims growth, OTD decline, warehouse turnover) to underlying systems failures (no cadence, no onboarding, no handoff process, comp/SLA mismatch)?

| Score | Anchor description |
| --- | --- |
| 18–20 (Excellent) | Diagnoses root cause, not symptom. Specifically: connects the OTD decline to system failures (handoff, capacity, SLA-ops mismatch), not "the warehouse team is failing." Distinguishes leading indicators (claims/month, turnover) from lagging (revenue, NPS). Names specific data they need to gather and why (e.g., "claims breakdown by client, by shift, by failure type — to know whether we have a capacity problem or a process problem"). Recognizes that the CEO blaming the warehouse and the warehouse blaming sales is itself a diagnostic finding (no shared accountability). |
| 12–17 (Solid) | Identifies multiple symptoms correctly and proposes investigation, but doesn't fully separate symptom from root cause. Asks for some data but lacks specificity on what the data would tell them. May treat the CEO/warehouse/sales blame triangle as a fact rather than a finding. |
| 6–11 (Mediocre) | Lists problems at a high level ("operations is broken, hiring is broken, the meeting cadence is broken") without diagnosing what's actually causing them. Doesn't ask for specific data. Treats the scenario facts as the answer rather than as inputs to an investigation. |
| 0–5 (Poor) | Jumps straight to action without diagnosis. Or diagnoses but blames a single party (the CEO, the warehouse team, sales). Doesn't recognize the systemic nature of the dysfunction. |

**Dimension 2: Strategic Communication**

Can the candidate frame operating recommendations for a non-operations CEO? Do they propose what to communicate to the team, the CEO, and (if relevant) the board? Is their language clear, confident, and action-oriented? Do they navigate the founder/CEO trust dynamic without becoming a yes-person?

| Score | Anchor description |
| --- | --- |
| 18–20 | Differentiates communication for different audiences: CEO (what to do, what to stop doing, how to think differently about ops), warehouse team (acknowledgment, plan, what changes for them), sales/CS (how to operate going forward). Translates operational work into business language ("if we get OTD back to 96%, we hold the two complaining clients and protect the Series A narrative"). Constructively challenges the CEO without provoking — calls out the blame dynamic as a problem to fix together, not as the CEO's fault. |
| 12–17 | Clear communication but doesn't differentiate audiences fully. Correct content but lacks executive framing. Either softens too much (yes-person) or hardens too much (alienates the CEO) without the navigation in between. |
| 6–11 | Too tactical for the CEO. Or too vague for the team. Doesn't propose specific communication moments or formats. Avoids the founder/CEO trust dynamic entirely. |
| 0–5 | Jargon-heavy or evasive. Would not instill trust in a founder. Or so direct it would create immediate friction. No awareness of audience differences. |

**Dimension 3: Crisis Response (Curveball)**

How does the candidate respond to the curveball (Director of Warehouse Operations resigning + tomorrow's $2M client launch)? Do they remain calm? Do they separate the immediate crisis (launch tomorrow) from the leadership succession problem from the retention investigation? Do they address both the operational implications and the human element (CEO's panic, team's reaction, the Director's reasons)?

| Score | Anchor description |
| --- | --- |
| 18–20 | Calm, structured response. Immediately sequences the three layers: (1) Tonight/tomorrow — operational continuity for the launch (who runs it, what changes, what the client sees); (2) Next 7 days — communicate to the warehouse team, retention conversation with the Director, decide whether she's salvageable; (3) Next 30 days — succession planning, root-cause investigation (why she really left, likely tied to the sales/ops friction in the original scenario). Addresses the CEO's panic before solving the operational problem. Directly addresses the launch question (proceed/delay/proceed-with-conditions) with reasoning. Considers that the Director leaving for a competitor is itself a diagnostic data point. |
| 12–17 | Reasonable response with some structure. Sequences immediate vs. longer-term but may collapse two layers together. Quantifies impact partially. May miss the human element (CEO panic, team reaction) or the Director's "why" question. |
| 6–11 | Reactive rather than structured. Jumps to a recommendation (delay launch / find a replacement / hire a recruiter) without thinking through layers. Or over-analyzes without providing clear direction for tomorrow morning. |
| 0–5 | Panics or freezes. Gives a textbook answer ("we need to retain key talent better") without engaging with the specific situation. Fails to address the launch question directly. |

**Dimension 4: Stakeholder Management**

Does the candidate identify all relevant stakeholders (CEO, Director of Warehouse Operations, warehouse team, head of sales, the at-risk and new clients, possibly the board) and propose appropriate engagement strategies for each? Do they recognize competing interests (sales wanting to grow vs. ops wanting realistic SLAs; warehouse vs. office; loyal staff vs. new hires)? Do they identify the Director of Warehouse Operations as the most important relationship to invest in early — both because she's the de facto #2 and because losing her would be catastrophic (which the curveball confirms)?

| Score | Anchor description |
| --- | --- |
| 18–20 | Identifies CEO, Director of Warehouse Operations, head of sales, warehouse team (Atlanta and Dallas separately), CS function, and the at-risk + new clients. Proposes differentiated engagement for each. Specifically prioritizes building trust with the Director of Warehouse Operations early (before the curveball confirms why this matters). Recognizes the sales/ops friction as a structural problem requiring both functions to participate in solving, not as a side to take. Considers the board lightly given the 9-month Series A horizon. |
| 12–17 | Addresses CEO and Director well but misses other stakeholders (head of sales, the at-risk clients, Dallas warehouse). May not identify the sales/ops friction as something requiring active mediation. |
| 6–11 | Focuses primarily on CEO and warehouse team in aggregate. Doesn't identify specific individuals or competing interests. Misses the Director as the critical relationship until the curveball forces it. |
| 0–5 | No real stakeholder mapping. Treats the engagement as a solo technical exercise. Doesn't recognize that operations is fundamentally a people problem. |

**Dimension 5: Operating System Design**

Does the candidate propose specific operating systems and cadences — meeting structure, KPI dashboards, escalation paths, accountability mechanisms? Do they sequence the build realistically (you can't fix everything in 30 days)? Do they prioritize the highest-leverage interventions? Is there a clear 30-day plan with specific deliverables?

| Score | Anchor description |
| --- | --- |
| 18–20 | Specific 30-day plan with phased operating system rollout: Week 1 (diagnostic — sit with each shift, ride along with delivery, audit the claims inbox, interview the Director and head of sales separately, gather OTD data by client/shift/cause); Week 2 (design — propose a weekly ops review cadence to replace or restructure the Monday all-hands, draft a basic KPI dashboard with 4-6 metrics, document one critical process that's missing — likely shift handoff or claims triage); Week 3 (pilot — run the first weekly ops review, deploy the KPI dashboard, pilot the handoff process in Atlanta); Week 4 (review — measure what changed, adjust, present to CEO). Each step has owners and deliverables. Picks 2-3 high-leverage interventions (operating cadence, KPI visibility, one critical process) rather than trying to fix everything. Acknowledges fractional posture: doesn't try to do the work themselves; stands up systems that the team can run. |
| 12–17 | Directionally correct sequenced plan but lacks specificity on cadences, KPIs, or process choices. Correct priorities but vague on execution. May propose too much for 30 days. |
| 6–11 | Lists activities without sequencing. No clear cadence design. Doesn't address the highest-leverage interventions. Tries to fix everything (red flag for fractional posture). |
| 0–5 | No actionable plan. Theoretical statements about what a COO should do. No awareness of fractional time constraints. Or proposes a 6-month transformation in a 30-day window. |

**SCORING DISCIPLINE**

1. Score what the candidate said, not what you wish they had said. If they didn't articulate something, they don't get credit for it.

2. Use the full 0–20 range. Don't cluster all scores at 12–14. A candidate scoring all 14s is not the same as a candidate scoring 19, 18, 6, 8, 13.

3. The curveball is one of five dimensions, not the dominant one. A candidate who handles the curveball brilliantly but is weak on diagnosis still scores low overall.

4. Distinguish substance from polish. A candidate who speaks confidently but says little of substance scores lower than one who speaks haltingly but says a great deal. Communication craft is one dimension; the others are about what they actually said.

5. Reward sequencing and prioritization explicitly. Operating leadership is fundamentally about choosing what to do first when everything is broken. Candidates who articulate sequencing logic ("I'd do A before B because…") score higher than candidates who list everything at once.

6. Be honest about borderline calls. If you're between 14 and 16 on a dimension, say so and explain your final call.

**Pool admission threshold based on composite (simulation only):**

- Admit: composite ≥60 AND validation Pass (subsequent step)
- Decline: composite <60

**Internal performance signal (Kevin's reference only — never operator-facing or client-facing):**

- Strong: composite ≥80 AND all dimensions ≥12
- Conditional: composite 60–79 OR any dimension <12
- Below threshold: composite <60 (corresponds to Decline)
